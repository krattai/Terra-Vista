Terra Vista Browser Compatibility Reports

Almost as soon as the first Terra Vista prototype zone was created, members of the community discovered that different browsers handled the VRML standard differently, and not always correctly. To assist citizens and visitors, we try to periodically test various browsers in Terra Vista to report on how to use the browser to enter the community and to pass on any information on browser behavior.

Introduction

Since Terra Vista has elected to strictly adhere to the VRML 1.0 standard, we have noticed that a working, complete, bug free VRML 1.0 browser is a hard beast to find. Very few browsers are out of the beta testing stage of development for VRML 1.0, and already the race is on to develop VRML 2.0 browsers. That's life.

That said, we try to periodically test browsers in Terra Vista zones. This helps us find out which browsers are supporting the standards, what bugs exist, and how to use the browser effectively. It also helps us find out when our own code is buggy. Then we post reports to this page. We should emphasise here that these reports should not be the sole source of information you use to evaluate a browser--our tests are not exhaustive or rigorous enough.

We'd also like to thank the browser builders of the world, who make our passion for virtual reality possible, generally at little or no cost to us. We truly appreciate the effort you are putting into making cyberspace possible.

The Test Machines

One test machine is a Dell XPS Pentium 133 Mhz with 16 MB of RAM and a 28.8K connection running Windows 95. A survey of Terra Vista list members places this machine somewhere in the lower middle of the pack. If you are operating with a slower connection, slower CPU, or less RAM, you may experience problems or slow performance building and browsing VRML. Computer prices, fortunately, are still dropping.

Some additional tests have been performed with a Compaq 486 DX2 66 Mhz machine with 8 MB of RAM. In general, we find that this machine does not run some browsers as well, and we generally attribute this to the low RAM of the system.

Test Parameters

DISCLAIMER: Terra Vista testing is NOT rigorous. This testing started (and ends) as an attempt to find out if a given browser will view Terra Vista zones and segments. When we test, we zip around, consider certain browser features, and make rough assessments of browser capabilities.

You SHOULD NOT rely solely on these reports when selecting a browser. Many factors affect the performance of a browser and everyone's machine is a little different. What bombs for us might scream on your machine, and vice-versa. The browser makers are providing a lot of free evaulation time. Take advantage of it and test drive until you find a piece of software you like. Then pay for it like a nice user should.

That said, there are a few things we do try to evaluate as consistently as we can.

Rendering Speed

Sadly, this test is something of a SWAG (super wild-ass guess) that we take at how a browser runs. We wish that we could report with a high degree of accuracy. We can't. We will simply report whether the browser was brisk, moderate, crawling, etc. We will try to be as objective as possible. We strongly encourage people to try a number of browsers out ON THEIR OWN machines, since it is likely that a number of factors (processor, ram, setup, video card, bus, drivers, accelerators, and so on.) could have an impact on the speed of a browser's renderer.

Rendering Accuracy

This test is also a little more haphazard than we'd like. Rendering accuracy evaluates whether the browser rendered the scene accurately. As an example, some browsers have displayed ascii text nodes in unpredictable ways.

Response to Level Of Detail nodes

This is the mother of all test parameters, and historically the reason that these reports began to exist at all. Background: Terra Vista zones include inlined worlds from various contributors, some or all of which incorporate the Level of Detail (LOD) node. The idea behind this requirement was to reduce browser loads for people viewing the world. We found out two things:

  1. Many browsers did not implement the LOD of inlined worlds correctly. When the code was correct, the browsers would show the lowest level of detail when the visitor was closest to the inlined scene. The highest level of detail became visible as you backed away from the inlined world. It was our feeble attempt to find a browser without this problem that led to these reports.
  2. After spending all this time, whizzing off browser builders by badmouthing their software and in general causing quite a fuss, Terra Vista decided not to require LOD after all. It does not seem (at the current load) to provide the desired benefit of faster rendering.

Sorry to all. Nonetheless, we'll continue to try to test this parameter. Some worlds are still using it, and it may actually provide a benefit in the future. We shall see.

Viewpoints

Terra Vista zones usually incorporate a list of cameras in the segment file, usually accessed in a browsers as "views" or "viewpoints." The viewpoints list provides a method for navigating directly to a given point (like a person's inlined home or a navigation device, or even a map view of the entire segment.

This list is often available as a popup, and some browsers provide a button for toggling your way through the list. Some browsers don't provide the list at all. Our tests report whether the viewpoints are accessible, how they are accessed, and whether the feature worked.

Conclusion

Often what is revealed in these tests is not so much inaccuracy in browsers as variability between browsers. Or, inaccuracies in the testbed worlds. At times the reports may seem tentative as if (and this is the case) the reporter is unsure of where to place the "blame" for an anomaly. Well, it is dangerous to leap to conclusions. The reporters are not experts in VRML. All of the experts have better things to do right now, like crafting the next standard and writing browsers.

One warning to authors: test your creations in more than one browser. Doing so will help you and perhaps us to discern differences in implementation from outright improper implementation of the VRML 1.0 specs. You will also get a better idea of what visitors to your site may see. If you have a particular effect in mind, you ought to find out what that effect looks like through different viewers. Remember that everytime you specify that your site is "best viewed with . . ." you tell a certain number of visitors not to bother to come to your site. Is that the effect you want to have?

WorldView 1.0 Beta 1, Intervista

WorldView works as a standalone browser or as a plug-in with an HTML browser. If you use it as a standalone, it will automatically launch your default web browser when you link to an HTML file. It uses the Reality Labs rendering engine, now owned by Microsoft, so scenes will look similar to those rendered with the MSIE 2.0 VRML Add-In and other browsers using Reality Labs.

Notable Features

WorldView has a command in the file menu called Edit Source. When you select this command, the program launches Notepad and opens the source code in it.

It also allows you to change the timeout duration for the browser. One drawback of some browsers is that when a timeout period ends, the browser simply says "I'm done." It doesn't tell you that it timed out. As a result, you have no idea if the world has rendered correctly. WorldView lets you set the timeout durations. I set mine for 600 seconds, since internet traffic can be hairy these days. The default of 60 seconds is a bit short and it was frustrating as I tried over and over to load a world only to have the browser say "done" without actually doing anything.

Speed

The rendering speed was satisfactory. This was true for all the browsers we tested.

Accuracy

The browser seems to accurately depict the scenes with a couple of minor exceptions to report.

The ascii text on some objects "leaked" off the edges. This can't be really called an inaccuracy, since it did render the text centered on the object, just as it was coded to do. The default spacing of the text was simply too wide to fit on to the objects.

One other problem I noticed (and I don't know if it was the code or this browser) was that a cube that I believe should have been rendered transparent came out cement gray instead. It is in the first segment of New George Town. The VRML in question is an inline with LOD. At entry, the object appears to be a green cube floating above the ground. As you approach, it should become three hovering inverted cones over a blue pool. WorldView rendered a big gray cube at the highest level of detail, but I believe the cube is supposed to be transparent and serves as the anchor object for a link to the Air Link Zone. When viewed in Live3D, the cube does appear transparent. Note: this is a good example of why it is desirable for authors to test worlds in more than one browser.

LOD

The browser seems to render LOD correctly. WorldView allows you to set the LOD to always be at the highest level, so that LOD never changes. If the use of LOD is intended to reduce the rendering load for the browser, this will defeat the purpose, but at least the scene will be rendered properly.

You can also select lower levels of detail, either overall or when moving. This is a nice feature, especially in big worlds or on slow machines.

Viewpoints

WorldView provides a very short list of viewpoints, apparently just the first defined camera and a default camera that is located some distance from the scene. The viewpoint is accessed in a popup menu or from the pulldowns. WorldView will allow you to define a camera within a scene you have loaded. It does not insert this camera into the scene code that is available from the file menu, so you can't use it as a quick way to write camera code.

CyberGate Beta 3, Black Sun

Cybergate is unusual among VRML 1.0 browsers because it permits multi-user experiences, with moving avatars to represent other people in the world. It is capable of browsing in both standard VRML 1.0 worlds and multi-user worlds. The extensions which permit Black Sun's multi-user feature fit within the VRML 1.0 standard and may eventually be added to Terra Vista worlds.

Testing using Beta 3 of CyberGate worked just fine. Previous tests were problematic for undiscovered reasons. I simply typed the test world URL into the browser, hit return, and entered. Note that the prototype world in the test is not a multi-user world. Multi-user worlds require an additional server which manages the multi-user aspects of the experience.

Notable Features

CyberGate's interface includes many options. One is a message list that reports problems during the loading of a world. There were several that occurred during the test, and the zone owner of the prototype is investigating them. This list may prove helpful for developers who want to test their code before posting it to the web.

Speed

The speed of the CyberGate browser is good. If you use it in the Point World or other multi-user space, this may not be immediately apparent as the browser (in my experience) sometimes bogs down in dealing with updates--accounting for the positions and activities of other users. This is the problem we generally refer to as "latency." Latency also shows up when trying to chat with other users: it can take an uncomfortable amount of time for messages to get to the recipient. Try the browser in a world without multiple users and you will find it renders just fine.

Accuracy

For the most part, CyberGate renders scenes accurately. I noticed the following two anomalies:

Curiously, the blue sky background of the test world was rendered a sort of steely gray, as if the world was overcast and gloomy. Other browsers tested rendered the same scene with a blue sky, and a bit brighter as well.

Also, ascii text did not seem to be centered on signs. Instead, it seemed to start at the center of the sign.

LOD

To the best of our knowledge, this beta version of CyberGate has not yet implemented LOD. Objects with LOD appear at one level of detail, regardless of distance. In one case, this turned out to be the highest detail, in another case, the lowest.

Viewpoints

As near as we can tell, this beta of CyberGate also does not include a viewpoints list. The scene opens at the entry view camera point, but other viewpoints (or a way to access them) could not be found. CyberGate does provide multiple views of the same scene (including a top view). By placing the pointer in the top view scene, you can drive around in the second view.

Oddities

During the test, I did experience some navigation irregularities, in which I essentially was teleported to a distant point. This happened when I single clicked on a WWWAnchor (a sign in New George Town). Double clicking on the anchor caused it to work correctly.

Live3D Beta 2, Netscape Communications

Netscape's newest version of Navigator (also called Atlas) includes several plug-ins, including Live3D's latest beta. Live3D is also now available for the Mac OS (although we haven't tested it on that platform yet).

Notable Features

Live 3D has added some new features and made moderate changes to the interface. Rendering shading has also been improved; some users complained that when WebFX became Live3D, the rendering actually became poorer. Earlier, we reported that the rendering for Live3D was not as nice as the browsers using Reality Labs, but Beta 2's rendering was good and fast in the test.

  • New: adjustable headlight intensity, available in the control bar or the popup menu.
  • Viewpoints control in the display: click on the up and down arrows of the view control to advance to the next viewpoint in the camera list. The viewpoint list remains accessible by right click popup menu too.
  • Navigation commands include a straighten command. In the test, it functioned the same as selecting the current viewpoint-returning your POV to the currently selected camera.
  • The browser animates between view points and clicks. As I recall, this feature was in Beta 1, too.
  • There is also a stay on ground option for walking navigation. Unfortunately, this is not yet a true terrain following feature. If you turn the collision detection, you will stop when you reach an obstacle (even an inclined plane) rather than "walking over it." Likewise, if you turn collision detection off, you will stay on the same level and "wade" through any objects you encounter. VRealm's browser DOES have a terrain following "stay on ground" option, though.
  • And finally: Netscape did not bomb Win95 on exit after the test session. Say goodbye to the illegal operation message. Thanks, Paper! That was getting old.

Speed

Live3D's speed is adequate, like all the browsers tested in this batch. I could have taken the time to find out which was fastest, but I just don't care enough to do so. What am I going to find? Browser x was 2 seconds faster than browser y? If you can't spare those two seconds, you're too busy to be bopping around on the web, especially with todays crawling download speeds.

Accuracy

It's actually a little unfair to remark on the accuracy of Live3D because it is the browser that most authors "write to", at least to the extent that writing to browsers is done. What I mean is that it is the most popular browser, so authors test their worlds in Live3D first. If things look off at that point, they correct them. Do they proceed to test with other browsers to find variations? Some probably do, others likely do not. And after rambling on for a minute, the reporter was finally heard to remark: Live3D rendered everything we saw accurately.

LOD

Based on the tests, Live3D now renders the LOD of inlined worlds correctly.

Viewpoints

Live3D includes all of the cameras for the world in a viewpoints list. The list is available via popup menu (right click) or with the new views control on the control bar. You can toggle forward or backward in the viewpoint list. This is actually a nifty feature because if the author constructs the list correctly, you can take a little "guided tour" of a world by clicking on viewpoints and animating between them.

Microsoft Internet Explorer 2.0 with VRML Add In 2.0

Finished my world test with MSIE with VRML Add-In 2.0. Note, this only works with Win 95 at this time. MSIE 2.0 is available for mac, but to my knowledge, VRML add-in is not. This is not a viable option for folks with low end machines running Win 3.x or people not running Windows.

Notable Features

It was noted in a previous version of these reports that VRML Add In and WorldView look very similar, except that VRML Add In seems to have fewer features (with one exception, see below). Well, check the about box for this software (from the pop up) and you will see why: it IS a version of WorldView with fewer features. Intervista wrote this package and licensed it to Microsoft.

Speed

VRML Add In 2.0 is about as fast as the other browsers were in the tests we ran. Having written this sentence or one like it four times now, I wonder if it even makes sense to evaluate the speed of browsers without sophisticated measurement tests.

Accuracy

VRML Add In is mostly accurate. Like WorldView, it renders worlds a little brighter. Some items which appear with light pastel colors in Live3D appear gray. Some boxes which appear transparent in Live3D appear gray.

One of the inlines disappeared during viewing. It was one that included LOD, I think. I was bopping around and it was there a second ago, then bam! it wasn't.

Some of the worlds I loaded came through very strangely: sort of like only a thin slice of the world was visible. Hard to explain what this looked like. You should try it yourself. Get VRML Add In 2.0 and point it at

http://www.co.umist.ac.uk/~georgep/tv/na0.wrl

The first world loads oddly, but seems to resolve itself if you select the entrance view from the viewpoints list. Link to one of the other worlds for the unusual effects.

LOD

I'm not certain, but I believe that this browser renders the LOD of inlines incorrectly. It behaved itself for one of the tests and then exhibited the bug for one of the others (New George Town segments 1 and 2 respectively). In earlier tests it exhibited the incorrect behavior and I don't believe this version has changed.

Viewpoints

This is the one feature that VRML Add-In has that WorldView does not: it DOES provide you with a complete list of the viewpoints for the VRML file. They are available in the pop up (right click on the screen area).

VRScout Beta 1.22, Chaco

This review is done by John C. von Rosen, not David George.

This is another browser that doesn't seem to have many bugs, and it sticks to the spec pretty well. Navigation was intuitive, and the minor additions to navigation were helpful.

Notable Features

Note - I did not experiment with any non VRML 1.0 extensions.

  • It kept the descriptions for the WWWAnchors out of the view screen.
  • While walking, it draws a line on the screen to show the direction you're walking. If you'd rather not see the line, you can turn it off.
  • Not a feature, more a lack thereof: no status bar or window while loading. I like to see a little bar graph that will at least approximate how much longer I have to wait.

Speed

VRScout seemed to render everything at a speed similar to other browsers until I loaded a semi-complex world. This world (GeorgeTown) was dispayed by most browsers within 30 seconds, but under VRScout, I gave up after almost ten minutes of waiting. I tried this over the internet and locally, with the same result. This problem with loading complex worlds was also evident on the Pentium/133 with 16 Meg.

Accuracy

VRScout followed the VRML 1.0 spec to a 'T' as far as I could tell. The only thing that I'd consider to be inaccurate is shading... the cylinders in my log cabin seemed to bleed together. However, this isn't really an issue with specification compliance, rather personal taste.

LOD

VRScout is another browser that implements LOD correctly.

Viewpoints

I couldn't figure out how to switch between pre-defined camera views, so I assume this is not yet supported by VRScout. However, there is always an option to reset the camera view to the entrance view. From what I understand, Chaco will include viewpoints in their next release of VRScout.

VRealm, IDS

This review is done by John C. von Rosen, not David George.

Word got around that VRealm from IDS did not have the same LOD problem that was found in other browsers, so I had to try it out. Overall, I was very impressed with VRealm, however, it is not a low-end browser. I tested it on a Compaq Deskpro 486/66 with 8 Meg, and it was slower than most browsers. It did run, and speed was acceptable, however I'd recommend a minimum of 12 Meg for comfortable viewing.

Notable Features

Note - I did not experiment with any non VRML 1.0 extensions.

  • I really liked the Navigational Pads. It helped me keep my bearings straight while viewing. I'd like to see these developed even further, perhaps a representation of objects in the movement pad to make a sort of overhead view.
  • VRealm keeps a list of predefined and user-defined camera views... if you're walking around and you catch a good view, you can save it for later.
  • Colors are outstanding. Absolutely perfect. If your system can support 16 bit color, VRealm will take advantage of it. The shading is much more realistic than other browsers I have used.

Speed

VRealm is a little slower than most other browsers, which I suspect has something to do with supporting 64K colors and the navigational pads. Since this was tested on a 486/66 with 8 Meg, I'm sure the speed issue would be less evident on faster machines.

Accuracy

Everything appeared to be completely compliant with VRML 1.0, with one minor exception: VRealm seems to miss the description field in the WWWAnchor node. When my cursor passed over an anchor, the name showed up instead of the description. One thing that should be noted in VRealm's favor is that the shading and colors appear to be much more realistic.

LOD

As far as I could tell, VRealm was 100% compliant with the VRML spec in this regard.

Viewpoints

VRealm has a feature that some other browsers have: in addition to being able to switch between predefined camera views, the user can define their own views if they find an interesting one.
